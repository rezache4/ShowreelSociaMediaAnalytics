# Refined taxonomy of CamiHawke's Instagram community

## Goal

This refined version updates the taxonomy after manual inspection of short comments, caption-dependent jokes, and tag-based comments.

The key correction is simple:

- many short comments are not really "unclear"
- they become classifiable once pragmatics and caption context are used correctly

## Guiding idea

The taxonomy still describes **ways of commenting**, not demographic types.

It remains anchored to Wang et al. (2025), but it now gives more weight to three things that are very common in CamiHawke's comments:

1. short admiration
2. contextual humor
3. public sharing through tags and mentions

## Final persona set

### 1. `affective`

Main motives:
`Authenticity`, `Sharing`, `Similarity`

What it captures:
warmth, symbolic closeness, affectionate admiration, praise of Cami as a person or relational presence. It also absorbs brief compliments marked by heart-eyes or "in love" emojis.

### 2. `identification`

Main motives:
`Similarity`, `Authenticity`, `Learning`

What it captures:
mirroring, self-recognition, "same", "this is me".

### 3. `humorous`

Main motives:
`Entertainment`, `Boredom`, `Sharing`

What it captures:
laughter, irony, punchlines, amused reactions, and caption-dependent jokes.

### 4. `aesthetic`

Main motives:
`Beauty`, `Aesthetics`

What it captures:
comments focused on appearance, attractiveness, styling, or visual scene when the main focus is visual rather than a short affective appreciation.

### 5. `informational`

Main motives:
`Information`, `Expertise`, `Learning`

What it captures:
requests for concrete references, titles, names, links, products, places.

### 6. `validating`

Main motives:
`Learning`, `Authenticity`, `Information`

What it captures:
endorsement of a point, reflection, or message.

### 7. `argumentative`

Main motives:
`Sharing`, `Information`, `Similarity`

What it captures:
explicit positions, opinions, or reasoning.

### 8. `autobiographical`

Main motives:
`Similarity`, `Sharing`, `Authenticity`

What it captures:
personal stories and lived experiences.

### 9. `advisory`

Main motives:
`Information`, `Expertise`, `Sharing`

What it captures:
suggestions, recommendations, orientation.

### 10. `situated_experts`

Main motives:
`Expertise`, `Information`, `Authenticity`

What it captures:
comments grounded in a declared role or competence position.

### 11. `defensive`

Main motives:
`Sharing`, `Authenticity`, `Similarity`

What it captures:
defense of Cami, the post, or conversational boundaries.

### 12. `sharing`

Main motives:
`Sharing`, `Similarity`, `Entertainment`

What it captures:
tagging, summoning, recommending the post to someone else, or using the comment as a public forwarding move.

### 13. `social_circle`

Main motives:
`Authenticity`, `Curiosity`, `Sharing`, `Entertainment`

What it captures:
close-circle comments, insider lore, recurring familiarity, repeated knowledge of Cami's traits, running details, and fan-side insider talk.

## What changed conceptually

Three refinements were necessary.

### 1. `other_unclear` had been inflated by short praise

Comments like:

- `Fantastica 😍`
- `Va bhe ciaone 😍`
- `meravigliosa, spiritosa, stupenda`

should not automatically be treated as unclear. In most cases they are better read as `affective`, especially when they are paired with heart-eyes or "in love" emojis.

### 2. Caption context matters for humor and visual reactions

Some comments only make sense if the caption is considered.

For example:

- a short ironic reply to an object mentioned in the caption
- a brief visual reaction like `che spettacolo 😍`

Without caption, these look generic. With caption, they become classifiable.

### 3. A distinct `sharing` persona was missing

Comments that mainly tag another user, invite someone in, or say in effect "look at this" were previously being forced into `other_unclear` or misread as `social_circle`.

That was a conceptual gap, because this behavior directly reflects Wang et al.'s `Sharing` motivation.

## How `social_circle` is now interpreted

`social_circle` is now broader than "real-life friends only".

It includes:

- actual close-circle creators and friends
- clear insider lore
- repeated familiarity with recurring traits or running details tied to Cami

So a comment can count as `social_circle` even if it comes from a follower, as long as it presupposes recurring and specific familiarity rather than generic fandom.

## Caption policy

The caption should not override the obvious meaning of a comment.

But it should be used whenever the comment is:

- short
- referential
- joke-like
- visually reactive
- tag-based and context-dependent

If the caption resolves the ambiguity, the comment should be classified rather than left in `other_unclear`.

## Final synthesis

The updated taxonomy still keeps the community readable at a high level, but it now handles three very common behaviors better:

- brief admiration
- contextual joking
- public sharing through tags

In short, CamiHawke's community does not only react, identify, and validate. It also very often:

- admires in compressed form
- jokes through context
- circulates the post socially by bringing other people in
